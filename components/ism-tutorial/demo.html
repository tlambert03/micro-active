Image Scanning Microscopy

How ISM, Airyscan, and Re-scan microscopy achieve super-resolution

About This Tutorial

Image Scanning Microscopy (ISM) is a family of techniques — including Zeiss Airyscan, Re-scan confocal microscopy, and Instant SIM — that achieve up to √2× better resolution than conventional widefield microscopy without sacrificing signal. This tutorial walks through the three key insights that make ISM work.

Key Equations

Airy disk (point spread function):

$$ I(r) = \left[ \frac{2 J_1(\pi r)}{\pi r} \right]^2 $$

where $r$ is in Airy Units (AU), with first zero at $r = 1$ AU ($= 0.61\lambda / \text{NA}$).

Confocal PSF:

$$ h_{\text{conf}}(r) = h_{\text{exc}}(r) \cdot \left[ h_{\text{det}}(r) \otimes P(r) \right] $$

where $P(r)$ is the pinhole function. As $P \to \delta$, the PSF narrows to $h_{\text{exc}} \cdot h_{\text{det}} \approx h^2$, giving √2 improvement.

Off-axis effective PSF:

$$ h_{\text{eff}}(r; d) = h_{\text{exc}}(r) \cdot h_{\text{det}}(r - d) $$

Peak is at $\approx d/2$ — the image shifts by half the pinhole displacement.

ISM reconstruction (pixel reassignment):

$$ h_{\text{ISM}}(r) = \sum_{k} h_{\text{eff}}(r + d_k/2;\, d_k) $$

Shift each detector element's image back by $d_k/2$ before summing. Result: narrow PSF (like closed pinhole) with high signal (like open pinhole).

Terminology

AU (Airy Unit)
Distance from the center to the first zero of the Airy pattern: $0.61\lambda / \text{NA}$. A standard confocal pinhole of "1 AU" diameter passes most of the central Airy disk.
Pixel Reassignment
The process of shifting each detector element's image by half its offset before summing, to produce a sharp combined image.
Airyscan
Zeiss's implementation using a 32-element hexagonal GaAsP detector array in a point-scanning confocal, with computational pixel reassignment.
Instant SIM
An optical (analog) implementation of pixel reassignment using microlens arrays to optically shrink the emission pattern by 2×.
